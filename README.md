## Overview

The **Rokurokubi** is a fascinating Japanese yokai that exists in **two distinct forms**, both involving transformation of the neck and head:

### Rokurokubi (Stretching Neck)
The classic form appears as a **normal human by day**, but at night their **neck extends to extraordinary lengths**, allowing their head to move independently while their body remains stationary.

### Nukekubi (Detachable Head)
The more dramatic variant—the **head detaches completely** from the body and **flies around** at night, leaving the body behind.

**The Hidden Affliction**: Many Rokurokubi are **unaware of their condition**, believing their nighttime activities to be mere dreams. Others are fully conscious and may be malicious, using their abilities to spy on people, drink lamp oil, frighten sleepers, or even drink blood.

**Fatal Vulnerability**: The Nukekubi face unique danger—if their **body is moved or hidden** while their head is detached, they will **die**.

This condition is often explained as a **curse or punishment for sins**, particularly Buddhist transgressions.

## Etymology & Names

### Primary Name
- **Rokurokubi (轆轤首)** — 'Rokuro' (轆轤) refers to a pulley or windlass (suggesting the extending neck motion), 'kubi' (首) means neck or head.

### Alternative Names

| Name | Language | Meaning |
|------|----------|---------|
| Nukekubi (抜首) | Japanese | "Removable neck" or "detachable head" |
| 轆轤首 | Japanese kanji | Pulley neck (Rokurokubi) |

## Characteristics

### Two Types

#### Type 1: Rokurokubi (Stretching Neck)
- **Day**: Appears as completely normal human
- **Night**: Neck extends to great lengths
- **Head**: Remains attached but moves independently
- **Body**: Stays in one place (usually sleeping position)

#### Type 2: Nukekubi (Detachable Head)
- **Day**: Appears as completely normal human
- **Night**: Head fully detaches from body
- **Head**: Flies around independently
- **Body**: Remains behind, vulnerable
- **Fatal Weakness**: Dies if body moved while head detached

## The Curse

### Origins of the Condition

The Rokurokubi condition is traditionally explained as:

1. **Curse for Buddhist Transgressions**
   - Punishment for breaking Buddhist precepts
   - Consequence of violating religious vows
   - Result of serious sins

2. **Hereditary Curse**
   - Inherited from cursed ancestors
   - Passed through family lines
   - Generational punishment

3. **Karmic Retribution**
   - Result of actions in past life
   - Buddhist karmic consequences

### Living with the Curse

**Unaware Rokurokubi**: Many have no idea they are yokai. They:
- Live completely normal lives by day
- Experience nighttime activities as strange dreams
- May have unexplained fatigue or memories
- Discover the truth accidentally or never at all

**Aware Rokurokubi**: Those who know their nature:
- May be horrified by their condition
- Some embrace their abilities
- Others seek to cure or hide their affliction
- A few become malicious, using powers deliberately

## Folklore Tales

### The Inn Keeper's Secret

A classic Edo period tale tells of travelers who stay at an inn where the innkeeper's wife is a Nukekubi. At night, her head flies around spying on guests. When one clever traveler discovers her sleeping body and moves it, the flying head returns but cannot reattach. The innkeeper's terrible secret is revealed as his wife dies from the separation.

### The Lamp Oil Mystery

In some stories, households notice their lamp oil mysteriously depleting overnight. They eventually discover a family member is a Rokurokubi, unconsciously drinking the oil with their extended neck during sleep—a harmless but eerie manifestation of the condition.

## Comparative Mythology

### Southeast Asian Parallels

The Rokurokubi/Nukekubi share striking similarities with Southeast Asian creatures:

**Penanggalan (Malaysian)**:
- Female with detachable head
- Head trails organs and intestines
- Drinks blood, targets pregnant women
- Body kept in vinegar

**Manananggal (Filipino)**:
- Upper body detaches from lower half
- Flies at night seeking blood
- Vulnerable if lower body is salted/moved
- Targets pregnant women and fetuses

These similarities suggest either:
- Cultural exchange between regions
- Parallel development of similar folklore motifs
- Ancient shared mythological roots

## Theories & Explanations

### Folkloric Interpretations

1. **Buddhist Curse**
   The condition results from punishment for breaking Buddhist precepts or vows—a physical manifestation of spiritual transgression.

2. **Hereditary Curse**
   Some traditions suggest it can be inherited, passing through family lines as ancestral punishment.

### Psychological Interpretation

**Sleep Disorder Metaphor**: The Rokurokubi may represent:
- Sleep disorders (sleepwalking, sleep paralysis)
- Dissociative experiences
- Activities during sleep one doesn't remember
- The unconscious mind "detaching" during sleep

The unawareness many Rokurokubi have of their condition mirrors how sleepwalkers don't remember their nighttime activities.
